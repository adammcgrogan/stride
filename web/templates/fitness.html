How to read this

Think of training like a bank account. Every workout deposits fitness but also charges fatigue. Your form on any given day is roughly how much fitness you have left after paying off the fatigue.

Fitness (CTL) — Chronic Training Load: a 42-day exponentially weighted average of your daily training load. It rises slowly with consistent work and drops gradually when you rest. A steady upward trend means you are genuinely getting fitter.
Fatigue (ATL) — Acute Training Load: the same calculation but over just 7 days, so it reacts quickly. It spikes after a big week and falls fast with rest. When Fatigue is well above Fitness you are digging a hole.
Form (TSB) — Training Stress Balance: Fitness minus Fatigue. Negative means you are carrying tiredness. Positive means you have freshened up. Taper before a race to push Form into the +10 to +25 window.
Form < −10Overreaching — You have been pushing too hard. Rest now or risk illness and injury.
−10 to 0Fatigued — Normal during a hard training block. Do not expect personal bests right now.
0 to +10Fresh — Good balance of fitness and recovery. A solid state for hard sessions.
+10 to +25Race-ready — Peak form. Schedule your A-race or time trial here.
> +25Detrained — You have been resting too long. Fitness is beginning to slip away.
How training load is calculated

Each activity is converted to a single daily load number using whichever signal is available:

  1. Relative Effort (Strava's heart-rate-based suffer score) — used when HR data exists. This is the most accurate signal.
  2. Energy output ÷ 10 (kilojoules from a power meter) — used for cycling when a power meter is present but HR is absent.
  3. 50 points per hour of moving time — a rough fallback when neither HR nor power data is available.

The numbers shown are arbitrary relative units. They are only meaningful compared to your own history — not against other athletes.
